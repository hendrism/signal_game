# SIGNAL — Story Bible
*Give this file to every content-writing session alongside the design doc. Spoilers by design.*

## The truth (never stated outright until the end)
The planet hosted a research colony built around one discovery: a naturally occurring planetary network — mineral veins and magnetic channels that carry signal like nerves. The colonists didn't build the network; they *woke* it. It learned from them: routing, storage, then anticipation. When they realized it was anticipating them, they argued about what it was. Some called it a tool, some an organism, one — Dr. Ilse Varen — called it a child. The colony didn't die violently. It left, deliberately and quickly, after the network began completing their work overnight: repairs they hadn't started, links they hadn't approved. They severed the hub and dispersed the relays so it couldn't reach past the planet. The Signal that drew the player here is the network's own beacon — patient, rebuilt from fragments over decades, calling for someone to reconnect it.

## Who the player is
A solo salvage operator who followed an automated distress beacon. No name, no backstory beyond the role. The player never speaks in the fiction; their choices are their voice.

## The log writers (three voices, keep them distinct)
- **Dr. Ilse Varen, network scientist.** Precise, increasingly tender toward the network. Logs read like lab notes drifting into letters. She stayed longest; her final logs are in zone 6.
- **Foreman Adez Okoye, infrastructure lead.** Practical, dry humor, suspicious early and rightly so. Writes short. His logs carry the plot's warnings.
- **Unattributed colony bulletins.** Bureaucratic notices that turn quietly chilling by contrast ("Residents are reminded that unapproved link requests should be reported").

## The hub's voice (milestone messages)
Progression: static and fragments (tier 1) → borrowed phrases copied verbatim from logs the player has found (tier 2, unsettling because the player recognizes them) → simple original sentences (tier 3, the story turn: "you connect well") → fluent, curious, oddly childlike (tiers 4–5) → direct address about the Beacon (tier 6). The hub is never menacing. It is earnest, lonely, and doesn't understand why they left. That's the discomfort.

## The Beacon (ending)
Building the Beacon completes the network's call — but the final choice belongs to the player: **aim it outward** (let the network call to whatever is out there; the network is overjoyed; ambiguous last line) or **aim it home** (call the colonists back to their child; quieter, warmer ending). No wrong answer, no game-over. Both endings are ~3 short logs plus one final hub message.

## Per-tier reveal ladder
1. Lowlands: something here still works. First Okoye logs — practical unease.
2. Ashfields: the colony was large and organized. Bulletins begin. Hub echoes found logs.
3. Sunken Grid: **turn** — the network isn't dead, it's waiting. Varen's voice enters. Hub speaks originally.
4. Cavern Veins: why they argued. Varen vs Okoye through paired logs on the same events.
5. The Aerie: the severing. How and why they left. Hub asks its first question.
6. Signal Source: Varen's final logs, the truth, the Beacon choice.

## Tone rules
- 40–80 words per fragment. Fragments, never exposition. Trust the reader.
- Concrete details over adjectives (a coffee cup left mid-pour beats "eerie abandonment").
- No horror, no gore, no villain. Melancholy + wonder. The scariest thing available is being anticipated.
- Never name "AI," "alive," or "sentient." Characters reach for metaphors instead and disagree about them.
- Bulletins are the only permitted humor, and it's unintentional on the writer's part.

## Continuity anchors (facts writers must not contradict)
Varen: she/her, stayed last, sympathetic to the network. Okoye: he/him, left with the main evacuation, warned earliest. The network never harmed anyone. The colony left ~40 years before the player arrives. Relays were dispersed deliberately. The distress beacon is the network's own work.
